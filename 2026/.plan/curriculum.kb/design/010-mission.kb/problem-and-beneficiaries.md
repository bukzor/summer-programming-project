Abby (10, Scratch background) is enthusiastic about a Minecraft-flavored
game — she raised "Minecraft" broadly, Buck suggested shaping the project
around it, and she lit up. The crafting mechanic inside that frame is her
own explicit pick, made when offered the choice
(`../../../claims.kb/abby-chose-crafting-mechanic.md`), not assigned.

The honest exception is the tool: Python-over-Scratch is the adult's goal,
not hers. Her own unprompted stated preference is to stay on Scratch
(`../../../claims.kb/abby-prefers-staying-on-scratch.md`), and her consent
to try Python was reluctant, not eager
(`../../../claims.kb/abby-reluctant-trial-consent.md`). That's why this is
a bounded trial with a real exit ramp back to Scratch, not a unilateral
switch (`../../../claims.kb/resolved-next-tool-trial.md`) — see
`../060-deliverables.kb/session-01-blocks.md` for where the checkpoint
sits.

Generic programming tutorials lose kids before the payoff; Abby already
has a concrete goal and a mental-model head start from Scratch (variables,
loops, events, conditionals, lists) that most beginner material doesn't
get to assume.

Beneficiaries: Abby learns real, transferable Python and gains confidence
reading her own errors, on a game whose mechanic and details are hers to
call. The adult gets a reusable, low-prep teaching structure. Shared
project time is honestly more Buck's goal (the language) wrapped around
something Abby's genuinely into (the game) than something she assigned
herself — worth being straight about that asymmetry rather than
overstating it as entirely her idea.
