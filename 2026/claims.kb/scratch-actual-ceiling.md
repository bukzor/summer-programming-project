Abby is right that Scratch's ceiling is higher than it looks — custom
blocks, cloud variables, clones, and even simple physics/platformers are
all genuinely achievable, and plenty of surprisingly "intense" games exist
in the Scratch community. The real limits aren't about what a *game* can
do, they're structural and mostly invisible until a project gets big:

- **Sharing outside Scratch.** A finished project lives on the Scratch
  website (or offline editor); there's no built-in way to turn it into a
  standalone app/executable to send a friend who doesn't use Scratch.
- **Project organization at scale.** Big projects become "block spaghetti"
  — lots of scrolling and hunting, because there's no file/function
  structure the way text code has (`def`, imports, multiple files).
- **General-purpose ceiling.** Scratch can't reach outside its own sandbox
  — no talking to the internet/APIs, no libraries, nothing that transfers
  as a skill to non-game software later.

None of these block "making an intense game" this summer. They matter if
the goal (per this project's key insight) is specifically the text-syntax
transition, not just "make a fun game" — which is exactly the tension
session zero needs to surface with her, not resolve for her.
